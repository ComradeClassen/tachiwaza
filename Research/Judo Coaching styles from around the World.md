# The chair, the grip, and the throw: a judo coaching language and style bible for Tachiwaza

**Elite judo coaching happens in 5-to-10-second bursts during Matte pauses, using pre-coded shorthand that varies dramatically by culture — from Japanese coaches' quiet single-word technique calls to Georgian coaches roaring "fight, fight, fight!" while straining the stay-seated rule.** These cultural coaching voices map directly onto thirteen distinct national fighting styles, each shaped by a specific folk wrestling ancestor and producing recognizably different movement patterns, grip strategies, and tactical philosophies. What follows is a complete reference for building Tachiwaza's Referee class, Matte window instruction system, and prose voice — grounded in documented coaching behavior, named techniques, real match footage, and the actual words coaches use in the chair.

## PART 1 — Inside the Matte window

### The anatomy of a 10-second coaching moment

Research by Emerson Franchini (International Journal of Performance Analysis in Sport, 2013) established that elite judo follows a roughly **2:1 work-to-rest ratio** — about 23 seconds of active combat followed by 11 seconds of pause. A PMC study on Olympic judo measured pause times more precisely at **5.1–6.8 seconds**, with semifinal bouts skewing longer (~6.8s). A typical four-minute contest contains **8–15 Matte-Hajime cycles**, each one a window for the coach.

The rules constrain the moment sharply. Coaches may communicate **only during Matte** — coaching during live action earns a warning, then ejection. Coaches must remain seated throughout (the USJF Code of Conduct, April 2025, states explicitly: "Coaches may engage with their athlete only during Matte"). No clapping, no filming, no disputing referee calls. A Canadian provincial coach was once warned simply for clapping during golden score. The EJU specifies that coaching is "strictly limited to communication with athlete, tactical advice, encouragement, instructions in unexpected situations, as injury."

The paradox: spectators in the stands have more freedom than coaches in the chair. They can shout during live action, stand, yell, and film. The coach, who actually knows what to say, is the most constrained person in the venue.

Within the 5–10-second window, an elite coach must accomplish four things in sequence: read the score-time-penalty situation (1–2 words), diagnose the tactical problem (grip, positioning, tempo, technique selection), deliver **2–5 words of actionable instruction**, and calibrate the fighter's emotional state (calm down versus fire up). The entire exchange rarely exceeds three sentences.

### What coaches actually say: a taxonomy of Matte calls

The instruction taxonomy breaks into seven functional categories, each with characteristic phrasing:

**Score and time status calls** are the highest priority: "You're up!" / "Shido!" / "Two minutes!" / "One minute!" These orient the fighter to the strategic situation. A coach who sees their fighter ahead on waza-ari with ninety seconds left delivers fundamentally different advice than one whose fighter is down on penalties in golden score.

**Grip commands** use Japanese terminology universally: "Kumi kata!" (get your grip), "Hikite!" (pulling hand / sleeve), "Tsurite!" (lifting hand / lapel), "Eri!" (collar), "Break the grip!" / "Cross grip!" / "High grip!" These are the most technically specific calls and vary least across cultures because gripping vocabulary is standardized in Japanese worldwide.

**Technique calls** are simply throw names, shouted as single words: "Seoi!" / "Uchi mata!" / "O soto!" / "Ko uchi!" / "Ashi waza!" / "Tokui waza!" (your best technique). The Kodokan head coach Makishi-sensei documented the command "Waza wo kakeru!" (apply technique) as a standard instruction. The coach is not teaching — they are reminding the fighter which weapon to reach for.

**Tactical direction** runs slightly longer: "Attack left side!" / "Ne waza!" (go to ground) / "Combination!" / "Turn in!" (for forward throws) / "Sweep!" (for foot techniques). These reorient the fighter's attack vector.

**Tempo and energy calls** are single words that regulate pace: "Tempo!" / "Pressure!" / "Move!" / "Circle!" / "Breathe!" / "Go!" / "Sen!" (initiative — take first action) / "Tai sabaki!" (move your body). These are as much emotional regulation as tactical instruction.

**Defensive warnings** signal imminent danger the fighter may not see: "Block!" / "Posture!" / "Head up!" / "Don't let him turn in!" / "Stand up!" (don't accept ground position). The highest-priority coaching call in any language is warning about an armbar or choke being set up behind the fighter's line of sight.

**Motivational calls** vary most by culture: "Come on!" / "Let's go!" / "You've got this!" — or in Georgian, a full-throated roar that functions as pure emotional fuel regardless of semantic content.

The coaching research literature (BJJ Intensive Camp guides, transferable to judo ne-waza) emphasizes **positive framing** — "Stand up!" processes faster under adrenaline than "Don't stay down!" The brain under combat stress handles negatives poorly.

### How coaching voice changes across cultures

**Japanese coaches are quiet, formal, and minimal.** The sensei-student relationship involves, as judoka Masashi Ebinuma described it after studying coaching in the UK, "some distance between judoka and coaches." Performance analysis happens separately; the coach delivers conclusions rather than collaborating. Ebinuma noted: "In the UK and all over Europe the relationship seems much closer... coaches and athletes do this work together, discussing strategy and practising solutions." Japanese matside calls tend toward single technique names and brief tactical cues in Japanese. The emotional register is measured, controlled, and fundamentally restrained. Under Kosei Inoue, this tradition continued but with modernized analytical methods — the coaching voice remained calm even as the preparation became more data-driven.

**French coaches are tactical, collaborative, and moderately vocal.** The INSEP system produces coaches who speak in precise tactical vocabulary. Patrick Roux, who coached the French, British, and Russian women's teams across his career, advocates a "cognitive training approach" — developing a "shared precise vocabulary between coach and athlete" through collaborative analysis rather than top-down instruction. Roux specifically criticizes coaches who impose generic calls: he describes a coach yelling "raise your hand" to an athlete who specializes in morote seoi-nage, which requires both hands low — "The coach blocks the athlete's judo." French coaching language mixes French tactical phrases with Japanese terminology, delivered with analytical precision.

**Georgian coaches are intense, loud, and physically demonstrative.** The coaching culture mirrors the fighting style: full commitment, maximum emotional engagement. Georgian head coach Lasha Gujejiani captured the essence: **"This is the Georgian style — we fight, fight, fight!"** and "We have written history with our hearts, and we are the best!" The 2024 Paris Olympics incident where Guram Tushishvili was ejected from the entire Games after reacting aggressively to a loss against Riner reflects the cultural emotional intensity. Georgian matside coaching strains the stay-seated rule. The emotional register runs hot, with motivational commands dominating over technical specificity. The coach's physical presence — leaning forward, hands gripping the chair, voice carrying — is itself a communication.

**Brazilian coaches are vocal, emotional, and family-oriented.** Rafaela Silva's 2016 Olympic final in Rio featured "the most vociferous of crowds" with the stadium "shaking and screaming." Brazilian coaching culture embeds emotional bonds — Silva described her coach Flavio Canto as someone who "looked after us like we were his children." Brazilian matside calls emphasize ne-waza opportunities more frequently than other traditions, reflecting the ground-fighting heritage. The coaching voice blends Japanese technical terminology with Portuguese emotional expressiveness.

**Russian coaching under Ezio Gamba was technically precise but psychologically liberating.** Gamba, the Italian Olympic champion who coached Russia to three gold medals at London 2012, described his approach: "I don't have to ask anyone, they follow what I say without questions, very committed." London 2012 champion Mansur Isaev explained: "In terms of technique, the head coach does not interfere in our work. He will only correct or suggest something. The atmosphere changed, the team has become very friendly." Russian coaching carries technical specificity from the sambo crossover — calls may reference specific grip configurations or wrestling-influenced positioning.

**Korean coaching emphasizes timing and explosive entry.** Korean fighters are drop seoi-nage specialists who need split-second entry timing. The coaching voice focuses on rhythm, entry moments, and speed cues. The training system emphasizes explosive speed over verbal elaboration.

**Mongolian coaching** draws on the bökh wrestling tradition. Julien Brulard (Olympics.com) noted that "Mongolian judoka bring some techniques that others have never seen before." The coaching tradition reflects the warrior culture — direct, physical, and intensity-driven.

**Cuban coaching under Ronaldo Veitía Valdivié** was methodical and scientific despite minimal resources. A Belgian coach visiting Veitía's training facility "was astonished to find only a bar, a rope, a ladder and a triceps-building machine. All of this equipment was set up outside, under a tree." Veitía's philosophy: **"The secret lies in the planning, frequency and scientific focus of the methods."** Cuban matside coaching combines explosive motivational energy with calculated tactical instruction — power with finesse, matching the fighting style.

### The Hajime-Matte coaching model

The closest existing framework to a formalized matside coaching system is the **Hajime-Matte Model** developed by Sensei Nick Lowe. It breaks the contest into distinct phases with a trademark sequence: **DKK (Dominate Kumi-Kata) → TFI (Throw For Ippon) → WIN**. This maps directly onto what a coach communicates during Matte: first, how to win the grip war; then, which throw to execute; then, the score situation and adjustments. The model provides an instruction taxonomy that could structure Tachiwaza's coaching AI.

Rhadi Ferguson, 2004 Olympian who helped develop game plans for double Olympic champion Kayla Harrison, uses systems dynamics to analyze matches — treating the referee, crowd, clock, competitors, and coaches as interconnected variables. This analytical frame suggests that the coach's Matte call should be sensitive to all five variables, not just the fighter's technique.

## PART 2 — Thirteen national fighting styles

### Japanese style: the progenitor school

Japanese judo is posture-based, built around the Kodokan curriculum's 68 recognized throws, with an ideological commitment to **ippon** — the perfect, decisive throw. The favored competition techniques are **uchi-mata** (inner thigh throw, the quintessential Japanese weapon), **seoi-nage** variations (including the distinctly Japanese "floating elbow" morote seoi-nage), **osoto-gari** (Yamashita's signature), **harai-goshi**, and **de-ashi-harai** (prized as the epitome of timing mastery).

The grip is classical **hikite/tsurite** — sleeve and lapel. Japanese fighters seek this standard grip quickly and attack from it rather than spending extended time in kumi-kata exchanges. They are traditionally less comfortable in prolonged grip wars than Georgians or Mongolians. Under Kosei Inoue's coaching, the Japanese adapted by becoming more proactive in grip-fighting but still prioritize reaching their preferred configuration fast.

The tactical philosophy is **ippon-or-nothing** — but with a modern tension. After Japan's disastrous London 2012 (zero men's golds), Inoue diagnosed the problem: Japan had been "obsessed with tradition and the style to gain proper grip and aim for ippon" while "foreign counterparts were constantly developing global judo by integrating their own martial arts and could apply techniques from any position." He modernized the approach while preserving ippon-seeking as the core ideal, producing five gold medals at Tokyo 2020. His philosophy for Ono: **"Assuming defeat and the worst-case scenario, while clearing each task."**

Shohei Ono embodies the modern Japanese ideal. Two-time Olympic champion with one of the highest ippon rates in history, specializing in osoto-gari and uchi-mata, bowing silently after victories without celebration. His statement: "What I wanted to demonstrate was that my style is the strongest judo style." His coach Kanamaru Yusuke on the pre-match moment: "I think a lot about what I say to Ono before competing. I'm always thinking about the best thing to say just at that last moment."

**For Tachiwaza**: A Japanese fighter moves with upright posture, seeks the classic grip quickly, and attacks with technically perfect throws. High ippon-probability, clean animations, potentially vulnerable to unorthodox grip fighters who deny them their preferred grip. The coaching voice is calm, single-word technique names, restrained emotion.

### Georgian style: power from the mountains

Georgian judo is the most distinctive national style in the sport, shaped fundamentally by **chidaoba** — folk jacket wrestling dating to the 9th century. High-level Georgian Olympic judoka have stated that **80% of Georgian judo is borrowed from chidaoba techniques**. The chidaoba jacket is sleeveless, forcing wrestlers to develop grips called **mochidave** — over-the-shoulder, around-the-back, and belt grips rather than sleeve grips. This directly produces the famous **Georgian grip**: the leading arm passed across the opponent's back/shoulder, leaving the opponent's head under the armpit, the hand gripping the belt or lower back of the gi. The rear hand grips the sleeve.

From this grip, Georgians attack with **the Khabareli** (obi-tori-gaeshi), named after 1980 Olympic champion Shota Khabareli. The mechanism: tori secures the over-the-top belt grip and hooks the attacking leg inside uke's leg (o-uchi-gari-like). This creates a devastating dilemma — if uke pulls back to resist being lifted, tori completes the o-uchi-gari; if uke doesn't resist, tori picks them up and rotates forward. Khabareli himself: **"The main thing that makes my technique unique is that you can get off balance any opponent, even the most technical."** Modern Lasha Bekauri variations abandon the trouser grip entirely, using belt control and upper-body rotation.

Other Georgian signatures include **sumi-gaeshi** (sacrifice throw variants from the over-the-top grip), **ura-nage** (Beka Gviniashvili's specialty — bear-hug entries into explosive rear throws), **makikomi** (Varlam Liparteliani's winding throws), and **tani-otoshi** (Eteri Liparteliani threw "a huge tani otoshi for ippon" at the 2025 Worlds).

Neil Adams on first encountering Georgian judo in the late 1970s: **"The style was so strange. It was more wrestling than judo. They were immune to our judo but we were not immune to their style."** He elaborated: "In tachi-waza, you had to be so careful not to get too close, because that would mean an immediate sentence, being thrown for a massive ippon. Then, the trickiest part was that Georgian competitors were so good at attracting you to get closer, that it was almost impossible to avoid it."

The Georgian grip war strategy is to bypass normal defensive structures entirely. Opponents trained in classical sleeve-lapel gripping are "simply not used to dealing and countering the chidaoba-influenced grips." They actively seek the over-the-shoulder position and will engage in aggressive wrestling-style clinch exchanges to get it.

**For Tachiwaza**: A Georgian fighter uses low stance, heavy forward pressure, and reaches for the belt and over-the-shoulder position rather than standard lapel-sleeve. Throws look more like wrestling slams — raw power, extreme angles, occasionally lifting the opponent bodily off the mat. Excellent counter-throwers: if you attack a Georgian and miss, you get punished. The coaching voice is loud, intense, motivational. Historically weak in ne-waza defense but modern Georgian judo has corrected this.

### French style: athletic tacticians from INSEP

France is the **largest judo nation outside Japan** — over 600,000 registered practitioners across 5,700 clubs. The system centers on INSEP in Paris, where "99% of the French national team" trains. The coaching creed emphasizes versatile fighting, intelligent rule exploitation, and physical dominance.

The French style is described by KL Judo analysis as "often associated with high-gripping and oftentimes, especially among the male players, a mauling style of judo reminiscent of the Georgians." This isn't coincidence — the French absorbed Eastern European influence. World Champion Stéphane Traineau "liked to grab the belt when doing his techniques." Djamel Bouras famously defeated Japan's legendary Koga at the 1996 Olympics "using this mauling style where he constantly grabbed the belt." As KL Judo notes: "If you look at the judo of Olympic silver medalist Larbi Benboudaud, you'll find it doesn't remotely resemble Japanese judo."

**Teddy Riner** — 2.04m, 137kg, triple Olympic champion, 11-time world champion, 154 consecutive match wins over a decade — epitomizes the modern French approach. His primary weapons are uchi-mata (leveraged by his enormous height), harai-goshi (he posted on social media calling harai-makikomi "one of my favorite movements"), and osoto-gari. His grip-fighting is tactical rather than forceful — **establishing dominant kumi-kata early and preventing opponents from gripping effectively**. Riner was criticized by the Japanese school for being "too tactical and not focused enough on spectacular ippon," but the French proved strategy is "a weapon as noble as all-out attack."

David Douillet, Riner's predecessor (double Olympic champion, four-time world champion), described the French approach as **"French flair in kimono"** — surprise, adaptation, rhythm changes.

Patrick Roux's coaching philosophy centers on long-term development: **"Build resources so athletes are performant at the Olympic Games, not chase junior medals."** He advocates what he calls the "slalom" approach — taking the longer, winding path to build skills rather than shortcuts.

**For Tachiwaza**: French fighters have high tactical intelligence, excellent grip-fighting, posture-breaking before explosive finishes. They can fight multiple styles. The coaching voice is analytical, precise, collaborative. Score managers who can finish when they choose to.

### Russian/Soviet style: the sambo hybrid

Russian judo is inseparable from **sambo** (SAMozashchita Bez Oruzhiya — "self-defense without weapons"), developed in the 1920s–30s by Vasili Oshchepkov, who trained under Jigoro Kano at the Kodokan. Critically, chidaoba techniques entered sambo and then re-entered judo through the Soviet system — Vladimir Volkov, who wrote the first Soviet state sambo course, devoted an entire chapter to chidaoba, stating **"in terms of technique and simplicity, chidaoba was the best."**

Russian judoka can "achieve an ippon from any grip — no matter how unorthodox." Before the 2013 leg grab ban, Russians dominated with **morote-gari** (double-leg takedown), **kata-guruma** (fireman's carry), and **te-guruma** (hand wheel). The leg grab ban eliminated a massive competitive advantage, but the Russian team adapted — at London 2012, they took the most gold medals ever in judo. Notably, "it is quite common to see Russians practicing these techniques in the dojo" even after the ban. The All Japan Federation partially reinstated leg grabs in October 2024 under conditions (must have upper body grip first), potentially reopening this door.

Under Ezio Gamba's coaching (2009–2021), Russia's team was "psychologically liberated." Gamba's Italian precision combined with Russian physicality produced three Olympic golds at London 2012. He was voted best coach in Russia across all sports — the first foreigner to receive this honor.

**For Tachiwaza**: Russian fighters are adaptable, comfortable from any grip, with strong ground skills from sambo crossover. Aggressive wrestling mentality. The coaching voice is technically specific. Post-2013, they've adapted but retain the DNA of the leg-grab era in their scrambling and unorthodox positioning.

### Korean style: the lightning school

Korean judo's defining technique is the **reverse seoi-nage** (the "Korean seoi-nage"), invented by 2008 Olympic champion **Choi Min-ho** — grabbing the same-side lapel with both hands and rotating away from the opponent. It was subsequently banned by the IJF for safety concerns. Beyond this, Koreans are the premier **drop seoi-nage** specialists, executing dramatic level changes with explosive snap-speed.

The distinguishing characteristic is **speed over power**. Korean techniques are described as "fast and powerful snaps" rather than force-based throws. An Baul was known for attacking with seoi-nage from a single-sleeve grip or during grip transitions — Koreans are "comfortable attacking from incomplete or transitional grips." They don't need the full classical grip to launch an attack, which makes them dangerous in the opening grip exchange.

Choi Min-ho's physical profile illustrates the Korean approach: 163cm, 60kg, but could deadlift **230kg** — overwhelming explosive strength-to-weight ratio channeled into speed rather than size.

**For Tachiwaza**: Korean fighters are compact, blindingly fast, and attack from unusual angles. Their signature animation is the explosive drop — suddenly disappearing beneath the opponent's center of gravity. Fastest attack speed, potentially less raw power per throw. They can explode into an attack from seemingly nothing. Vulnerable to power-grapplers who prevent their entry.

### Mongolian style: steppe wrestling in a judogi

Mongolian judo has the most direct folk-wrestling-to-judo pipeline of any national style. **Bökh** (Mongolian wrestling) has approximately 40 base techniques with up to 600 variations. The bökh zodog (open-chested jacket) forces waist/belt gripping, which transfers directly to judo. Bökh has no ground game — the match ends when anything other than feet touches the ground — explaining why Mongolian judo is heavily standing-focused. Bökh has no weight classes, teaching wrestlers leverage against larger opponents.

The grip is the key distinguishing feature. Mongolians use **over-under** (one arm over, one under), **double underhooks** (wrestling-style body locks), and heavy **belt grips** — all derived directly from bökh. They are less dependent on the standard hikite/tsurite than any other school. They dominate the grip war through physical pressure and unorthodox gripping, seeking close-range clinch positions where their wrestling base gives them advantage.

**Naidangiin Tüvshinbayar** — Mongolia's first-ever Olympic gold medalist (2008, -100kg) — grew up wrestling on the steppe as a nomadic herder's son, didn't try judo until age 18. He defeated reigning Olympic champion Keiji Suzuki in 86 seconds. **Khashbaatar Tsagaanbaatar** won major honors across four different weight categories (-60, -66, -73, -81kg), demonstrating the bökh-trained fighter's ability to compete across sizes. Mongolia's judo infrastructure was built by **Khaltmaa Battulga**, who later became President of Mongolia — himself a 1981 sambo world champion who personally trained with the national team.

**For Tachiwaza**: Stocky, powerful, forward-marching stance. Grip animations show reaching for belt and underhooks. Throws look like wrestling slams. Excellent counter-throwers. Vulnerable to speed fighters who avoid the clinch.

### Brazilian style: ground predators with Japanese roots

Brazil has the largest Japanese community outside Japan (~2.1 million people), and Brazilian judo descends directly from this diaspora. But the BJJ crossover creates something unique: it is "not uncommon for high level Brazilian judokas to also practice BJJ and for them to be highly proficient in both arts." Brazilian judoka treat time on the ground as an opportunity, not a risk.

**Flavio Canto** is the archetype — 6th-degree black belt in BJJ and coral belt in judo. His **Canto Choke**, a gi choke applicable from open guard, half guard, De La Riva, side control, turtle, and while passing, "took judo by storm and became a staple technique for elite competitors." Stories exist of him defeating BJJ world champions in ground sparring. Canto on what separates elite grapplers: **"It's not enough to be good on the ground, it is essential to know to fight at a time when most athletes do not go for it."**

Canto's social legacy shapes Brazilian coaching culture: he founded Instituto Reação, an NGO promoting judo in Rio's favelas. **Rafaela Silva** — 2013 World Champion, 2016 Olympic gold — grew up in Cidade de Deus favela and enrolled at Instituto Reação at age five. This family-oriented, community-embedded coaching model produces fighters who are emotionally connected to their coaches and fight with visible passion.

**For Tachiwaza**: Brazilian fighters have elevated ne-waza stats, willingness to transition to ground, flying attacks that flow into submission attempts. Classical Japanese-influenced throwing with additional ground mastery. The coaching voice is emotional, vocal, emphasizes ground opportunities.

### Cuban style: the Lamborghini school

As one analyst put it: **"If judo was a 0-60 drag race, Cuban judo would be the Lamborghini — pure power with some finesse, while other explosive judo nations would be a muscle car, all power no finesse."** Cuban judo blends explosive athleticism with methodical, scientific training under resource constraints.

The Cuban women's program is the most successful in judo history, built by **Ronaldo Veitía Valdivié** (1947–2022) — the most decorated judo coach in Olympic history. Across six Olympic Games (1992–2012), he produced **5 gold, 9 silver, 10 bronze medals**. In 30 years: 308 international medals. He took charge of the women's team in 1986 when they "had not even won a Central American or Caribbean title." His philosophy: minimal equipment, maximum results. On training women: **"The first thing to do was to change the opinion that exercises for men made women lose their femininity. It is amazing how many of them can now bench-press 90 and even 110 kg."**

**Driulis González Morales** — five Olympic appearances, four Olympic medals (including 1996 gold), three World Championship golds, eleven Pan American titles — is the greatest Cuban judoka. She "can grab her opponent's kimono with a man's grip." **Idalys Ortiz** continued the tradition with five Olympic appearances and four medals including 2012 gold, training seven hours per day. Ortiz: **"Winning Olympic gold didn't change me... the most important part has not been to win against my opponents but to win against myself."**

The Cuban system flows from Fidel Castro's declaration that sports were "the right of the people, not the right of the wealthy." Every school participates in judo through the Junior Olympic Program. Athletes train full-time as their profession. The coaching approach emphasizes **combination attacks** — sophisticated multi-throw sequences designed to exploit defensive reactions.

**For Tachiwaza**: High explosiveness, excellent conditioning/endurance, calculated combination attacks, power-plus-technique blend. The coaching voice combines motivational energy with scientific precision.

### Dutch style: technical giants from Geesink's lineage

Anton Geesink (1.98m, 120kg) shattered Japan's monopoly by winning the 1964 Tokyo Olympic gold at judo's Olympic debut. He won by **kesa-gatame** (scarf hold) in the final, demonstrating that size combined with technique could overcome traditional Japanese dominance. He also won 21 European Championship titles, 3 World Championships, and was a three-time Dutch national champion in Greco-Roman wrestling.

The legacy: Dutch judo maintained a tradition of technically proficient bigger fighters. **Wim Ruska** won two golds at a single Olympics (1972). **Mark Huizinga** won the 2000 Olympic gold, five European titles, and twelve total European medals with classical technique and strong osaekomi (pinning). **Henk Grol** carried the heavyweight tradition into the modern era. **Noël van 't End** won the 2019 World Championship.

**For Tachiwaza**: Technical and methodical, clean execution at all sizes, strong ground game especially in pins. Classical gripping with precision execution.

### British style: the ground game as identity

Neil Adams (1981 World Champion, double Olympic silver, now 9th dan) defined British judo through his legendary **juji-gatame** (cross-armlock). The origin story matters: a painful loss on the ground at the 1978 British Open forced him to completely rethink his approach, transforming a weakness into his defining strength. This personal evolution mirrors the broader British identity — **the ground game as a path to excellence when you lack the massive federation of France or the cultural wrestling base of Georgia**.

The British tradition produced specialists who set standing-position traps that lead to groundwork. **Craig Fallon** (2005 World Champion) created the **"Fallon Roll" (hiza-gatame)** — a signature armlock — plus tomoe-nage-into-juji-gatame combinations. **Euan Burton** developed juji-gatame from sumi-gaeshi grip and ude-gatame launched from a "Russian arm grip," showing cross-pollination between standing and ground.

Adams on the urgency of judo's ground game versus BJJ: **"We have to build it and we have to build it quickly"** — calling groundwork **"that ten-second blast."** This is critical for Tachiwaza: judo ne-waza is not BJJ's patient positional game. It's explosive, urgent, and time-limited.

**For Tachiwaza**: British fighters are transition-focused, following through on failed throws, staying close, attacking immediately on the ground. Submission hunters (armlocks, strangles) rather than pin specialists. The coaching voice is tactical, analytical, ne-waza-opportunity-focused.

### German style: disciplined technicians

German judo is systematic and methodically prepared. **Ole Bischof** (2008 Olympic champion) represents the ideal: three drop seoi-nage variations including "an amazing reverse variation," plus sumi-gaeshi, ashi-sankaku-garami, and o-uchi-gari. His coach Frank Wieneke (1984 Olympic champion) represents the coaching lineage — Olympic champion training Olympic champion through structured, technically precise development.

German training features structured frameworks: Rahmentrainingspläne (framework training plans) and Grundkampfkonzeption (basic fight concept). Bischof's philosophy: **"It's about becoming better together, always being courageous, and maintaining the respectful, fair, Japanese code."**

### Uzbek/Central Asian style: the tactical calculator

Uzbek judo emerges from both **kurash** (3,500-year-old standing jacket wrestling) and the Soviet sambo pipeline. Kurash's key rule — standing only, no ground work — and its harsh passivity punishment (three warnings in 25 seconds without attacking = loss) explain the Uzbek fighters' constant attack rate and tactical pressure.

**Davlat Bobonov** (2022 World champion) demonstrated "a masterful lesson in tactics" — using early attacks to draw shido penalties, then maintaining pressure. Academic analysis shows high effectiveness across all throwing categories, suggesting versatility rather than a single-technique focus, plus 24.8% effectiveness in ground work — unusually strong for a Central Asian style.

The current head coach is **Ilias Iliadis** — the Greek-born 2004 Olympic champion of Georgian heritage — representing the hybrid nature of modern Uzbek judo: Georgian tactical knowledge grafted onto a Soviet-era training infrastructure with kurash-influenced standing power.

## PART 3 — When styles collide

### Georgian power versus Japanese technique: the eternal matchup

This is judo's most narratively compelling stylistic clash. The pattern: Georgian fighters close distance with the belt grip; Japanese fighters need space for clean throws. Georgian wins if distance closes; Japanese wins by finding "technical solutions" to gripping.

The 2024 Paris Grand Slam final at -73kg (Ishihara vs. Chikelidze) was literally headlined by the IJF as **"-73kg: Japanese and Georgian Styles Clash in a Final of Debutants."** The match report: "At first Ishihara struggled with the dominant Georgian gripping but that struggle was replaced by technical solutions and Ishihara applied a superb ashi-guruma attack." Once he found answers to the Georgian grip, "he grew in confidence and attacked more." Japanese technical adaptability overcame Georgian grip dominance.

But the reverse happens too. At the 2023 World Championships semifinal (-90kg), Georgian Bekauri faced Japanese ashi-waza specialist Murao. Murao struck first with uchi-mata for waza-ari. Bekauri responded 30 seconds later with "a speedy sumi-gaeshi to level the scores." Then "in the next exchange the Georgian threw again, this time with an unusual sacrifice throw; Murao couldn't believe what had happened." Georgian explosiveness overcame Japanese methodical technique.

### The mauling problem: how French-Georgian tactics neutralize Japanese speed

Japan's legendary Toshihiko Koga — arguably the greatest ippon-seoi-nage practitioner ever — was defeated twice by this mechanism. In 1988, Soviet/Georgian Tenadze "forced Koga into adopting an extreme right-handed stance, something he was not used to." Tenadze's belt grip overwhelmed Koga. At the 1996 Atlanta Olympic final, France's Djamel Bouras replicated the strategy: he "had clearly studied Koga's gripping style and basically mauled him the way Tenadze had done." Bouras later said: **"I lost against Koga in semi in Chiba, at the 1995 World Championships, but that also gave me power and I kept the image of that loss and trained with that always in mind."**

The lesson for Tachiwaza's AI: mauling/belt-grip style can neutralize even the most technically brilliant fighters if the mauler controls the grip and closes distance. But if the technical fighter maintains distance and scores early, the mauler must overcome a deficit — and Koga squandered exactly this advantage by "playing the defensive game, hoping to kill time."

### Georgian power versus Korean speed: 2025 World Mixed Team Final

Georgia defeated Korea **4-1** to become the first-ever non-Japanese nation to win the mixed team world title. At -57kg, the IJF noted that Huh and Liparteliani "could not be more different in style but the queen of the moment is without doubt Ms Liparteliani. She absolutely dominated her opponent, winning with a massive tani-otoshi." Georgian power and depth overwhelmed Korean speed across most categories. Georgian quote after the match: **"We are Georgians, we are the best!"**

### How the leg grab ban reshaped the competitive landscape

Before 2010, judoka from wrestling-influenced nations used morote-gari, kata-guruma, and ankle picks extensively. The progressive ban (2010–2013) eliminated these tools. The impact was asymmetric: Russia and Mongolia lost the most, while Japanese classical judo and Georgian belt-grip power — neither of which relied on leg grabs — gained relative advantage. At the 2016 Olympics, initial attacks had **11% success rate** while counter-attacks had **30%** — hugely rewarding counter-fighting styles. The French federation objected: "The rules of arbitration issued by the IJF do not match the image of the judo of our federation."

## PART 4 — Refereeing patterns and the Matte call

### When referees actually call Matte

Matte (待て, "wait") triggers in specific situations: **out of bounds** (any judoka leaving the contest area in standing or ground), **stalemate in standing** (static gripping without attack progression), **non-progression in ne-waza** (ground fighting stalled without meaningful advancement), **before awarding penalties**, **judogi adjustment**, **medical attention**, **safety situations**, and **after certain scores**. Before 1976, calling Matte did not automatically stop the clock — the referee additionally had to call "jikan" (time).

At IJF World Tour events, referees wear headphones connected to the **CARE system** (Computer Assisted Replay). Head Referee Directors sit at a technical table with replay monitors and communicate directly to the on-mat referee. Scoring and penalty decisions can be reviewed and corrected in real-time. The coach cannot challenge decisions — review is entirely within the referee commission's control. This means disputing calls is futile and will result in ejection.

### Shido escalation and penalty strategy

The current system: **three shidos equal hansoku-make (disqualification)**. Penalties never correspond to scores — shidos are tracked separately until the third triggers disqualification. In golden score, shidos carry over but a single additional shido does not decide the match unless it is the third cumulative.

What earns shido: **non-combativity** (the most common), **false attacks** (dropping to knees without moving the opponent), **excessive defensive posture** (generally more than 5 seconds), intentionally going out of bounds, illegal gripping (pants grips as kumi-kata without immediate attack), avoiding grip engagement.

What earns direct hansoku-make: head-diving techniques, intentionally trying to injure, kawazu-gake (reaping from inside), lifting and slamming a supine opponent, actions against the spirit of judo.

Three types of hansoku-make exist with different consequences: **progressive** (from accumulated shidos — athlete may continue in repechage), **"protection" direct** (dangerous technique — athlete may continue), and **"spirit of judo" direct** (unsportsmanlike conduct — full disqualification plus sanctions).

Reading the referee's tendencies is a key coaching skill. As Dr. Diba Mani (IJF-A Referee) notes: **"European referees often emphasize gripping initiative. Asian referees may be quicker on technical scoring. Some are stricter on passivity."** For Tachiwaza's referee personality system, this variation is the core design parameter.

The 2025 rule overhaul reintroduced **yuko** (for 5-second ne-waza pins), expanded below-belt grips, allowed bear grips, and re-allowed reverse seoi-nage for seniors — each change shifting the tactical equilibrium.

### Coaching around the penalty clock

UAE coach Victor Scvortov: **"We work hard to not get shido."** Coaches track penalty counts for both fighters and adjust strategy dynamically. If the opponent has two shidos, the tactical calculus shifts dramatically — the coach might instruct a fighter to maintain pressure and force the third penalty rather than risk a throw attempt. Conversely, a fighter with two shidos must attack or face disqualification, creating desperation that a skilled opponent can exploit through counter-throwing.

## Conclusion: design parameters for Tachiwaza

The Matte window instruction system should generate culturally-flavored 2–5 word commands from a taxonomy of seven call types (score/time, grip, technique, tactical, tempo, defensive, motivational), with cultural modifiers controlling volume, emotional register, technical specificity, and language mix. A Japanese coach personality should be quiet and technique-name-focused; a Georgian coach should be loud and motivational; a French coach should be analytically precise.

The Referee class needs three personality axes: **grip initiative strictness** (how fast they call shido for passive gripping), **scoring threshold** (how generous they are with waza-ari on borderline throws), and **ground time tolerance** (how long they let ne-waza develop before calling Matte). European referees should skew toward grip initiative enforcement; Asian referees toward technical scoring sensitivity. The CARE system means the referee is not fully autonomous — there's a shadow observer who can override.

Fighter archetypes should be built around the national style matrix: grip preference (classical/Georgian/over-under/single-sleeve), primary throw family (hip/shoulder/leg/sacrifice), tactical disposition (ippon-hunter/score-manager/pressure-fighter/counter-fighter), ground orientation (standing-only/transition-opportunist/ground-predator), and the folk wrestling ancestor that shapes their movement DNA. A Georgian grip-fighter who specializes in Khabareli and sumi-gaeshi should move and grip fundamentally differently from a Korean drop seoi-nage specialist who attacks from incomplete grips at lightning speed — and both should feel different from a Brazilian ne-waza hunter waiting for the moment to pull the fight to the mat.

The prose voice should be concrete, physical, and technically specific — using Japanese throw names naturally, describing the grip battle as positional chess, treating ippon as an existential moment rather than a score increment, and capturing the compressed desperation of the 10-second groundwork window that Adams calls "that ten-second blast." Mifune's line is the north star for the game's tone: **"A chance to try your technique is in one instant never to be regained, so try it without hesitation."**
